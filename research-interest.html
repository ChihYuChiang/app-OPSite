Research Interest

Connect games with players, methodically

Challenge 1

﻿To understand players,
​vindicate the linkage between player types, temperaments, and mental needs.

Challenge 2

To understand games,
​categorize game mechanisms by their mental effects and their connected types of fun.

Challenge Ext.

To connect games and players,
​combine the findings from
​Challenge 1 & 2, matching game mechanisms and users’ mental needs.

Background

Each year, worldwide gamers devote billions of hours enjoying positive experiences in games; the whales, "tuhao" players in China spend as much as 30 thousand dollars per title, contributing 81.5 billion dollars in 2014 to the global game market. Outside traditional boundaries, modern enterprises utilize storytelling, trophy collection, cooperation and competition to produce a “gameful experience”. Through using games, those techniques deliver an affluence of emotional and self-expressive values over and above the functional ones, making systems more effective, possibly with lower costs. Together with this trend, terms such as “gamification” have significantly gained popularity; opinion leaders such as Jane McGonigal have gathered millions of video viewings on platforms such as Youtube and TED in the last five years.

​While games are influential, our understanding of them is limited. With most academic attention focused on game technology, the design aspect of games is often overlooked. Relevant research is few and far between, let alone there being a formal design knowledge system. Although several scholars, including Marc LeBlanc (2004), Nicole Lazzaro (2004), and Pierre-Alexandre Garneau (2001) have identified the types of fun, pointing out that fun is “what” enthralls the players of games, few have demonstrated “why” people demand fun, and “how” it is created.

As a result, game studios do not know their players, and, in spite of a legion of prototypes, cannot really manage what they are bringing to the audience until the final products come out. Exceedingly many game designs, even the professional ones, depend heavily on “designers’ instinct”, which is shaky, making product quality unpredictable. This was the reason for Rovio’s IPO failure, and the plunging stock price of Zynga and King. Games’ market success is, therefore, very hard to replicate, and the most lucrative game companies are publishers rather than developers. Furthermore, people talking about gamification often stop in the early stages, omitting to leave sufficient design details to be followed up, in order to identify real improvements. This is the reason why a plethora of critics see bringing game mechanisms outside the games environment as being frivolous and impractical.​
Realizing the lack of systematic knowledge in game design, I conducted a literature review, exploring gaming models and psychology theories with my design and business experience, trying to piece together a logical picture of behind the game scene. A general framework, The Essence of Game Experience, was established from this effort. It visualizes the relationship between game elements and satisfying game experiences, and responded to the “why” and “how” questions, giving rise to two research challenges I aim to resolve in the next couple of years.
> ​​​​The Essence of Game Experience

Challenge

The first one is to vindicate the linkage between player types, temperaments, and players’ psychological needs—to know more about “players”. Some informal reviews have shown that, not surprisingly, Bartle (1996)’s player types can, to some degree, be matched to the long-established temperament models, such as Keirsey (1998)’s, and even the human needs hierarchies, such as that of Maslow (1943). A chained relationship is proposed between the three—the player types are actually manifestations of temperaments, which decide players’ specific psychological needs. The player types derive game preference, and, by playing preferred games, players satisfy those specific needs and acquire corresponding “fun”. This is the genesis of the values that game experience delivers, and the very reason “why” people demand games. In addition, with the relationship clarified, a more precise test for sorting players into player types will be developed in my subsequent research.
* Illustrated by framework, The Essence of Game Experience
The second challenge is to categorize game mechanisms by their effects, in order to answer “how” the different types of fun are made—to discover more about “game” itself. While together bringing about gameplays, different mechanisms, in fact, each possess specific effects on human minds. Some are utilized to trigger the sensation of competitive triumph, while some are employed to emphasize a feeling of growth. They each satisfy a different set of mental needs, and create certain types of “fun” for the players. To clarify what specific types of mental benefits can be produced by specific game mechanisms, a database is proposed, which categorizes game mechanisms by their unique psychological effects and their connected types of fun. From the perspective of knowledge management, it will be web-based and open-sourced for designers’ executive reference and, will have tags, mechanical descriptions, game examples, and origins for easy queries and searches.
Connecting “games” and “players”, and combining the findings of the two studies above, an algorithm will eventually be developed that provides suggestions for best matching target players and game mechanisms. The ideal process will use the test developed from the first challenge to analyze target players, acquire their player types, and use this algorithm to suggest the most appropriate game mechanisms to be used in the designs from the database developed in the second challenge that best respond to the specific players’ needs. After all, only the right match of mechanisms’ effects and players’ needs creates the desired fun and reveals the value. The process assumes the key to a better leverage of all game designs.

Impact

Game, as an interactive vehicle conveying unparalleled mental values, has the capacity to shape human behavior and has long been contained within immature game worlds. Consideration of the “why” and “how” questions helps make the right matches between game mechanisms' effects and players' needs. It helps designers to create more purposeful game developments that raise the general game quality, and gives a deeper understanding of player experiences, which untethers game design’s possible applications, not only in games, but also in real world challenges.
